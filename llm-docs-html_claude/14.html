Alignment: RLHF, DPO, and Instruction Tuning

Techniques for Aligning Models with Human Preferences

The Alignment Problem: Making Models Behave as Intended

Language models are trained to predict the next token given previous tokens—a task misaligned with what we actually want them to do. A model trained purely for next-token prediction might generate plausible-sounding disinformation, refuse helpful requests, output offensive content, or ignore instructions. This divergence between training objective and desired behavior is the alignment problem. Alignment techniques modify models to behave according to human values, preferences, and intentions.

The challenge is profound: how do you formally specify "behave helpfully," "refuse harmful requests," or "be honest?" These concepts are subjective, context-dependent, and sometimes contradictory. Different users have different values. What one person considers helpful, another considers manipulative. Alignment techniques must navigate this complexity, often accepting approximations and imperfect solutions rather than perfect alignment (which may be impossible).

Why Alignment Matters

Model behavior reflects choices made during training. An unaligned model might output: (1) factually false information it generates with confidence, (2) biased or stereotyped content reflecting training data imbalances, (3) offensive language or ideas, or (4) refusals to help on legitimate requests. These behaviors are problematic for real-world deployment. Users lose trust in systems that hallucinate, users with underrepresented backgrounds see bias, and systems that refuse legitimate help frustrate users. Alignment techniques aim to make models helpful, harmless, and honest—the core characteristics of trustworthy AI systems.

Key Insight: Alignment is not about censorship or control but about ensuring model behavior reflects human values rather than unintended side effects of training objectives.

Instruction Fine-Tuning: Teaching Models to Follow Instructions

Instruction fine-tuning is the simplest alignment technique: train models on instruction-following examples. You provide instructions (prompts) and desired responses (completions), then fine-tune the model to match. This teaches the model to recognize when a user is making a request and produce helpful responses rather than continuing text in unprompted directions.

The Mechanism and Effect

Most language models begin as base models trained purely on next-token prediction. Instruction fine-tuning fine-tunes these base models on a dataset of instruction-response pairs: question-answer, request-completion, etc. This shifts the model's behavior from "continue text in any direction" toward "respond helpfully to questions and requests." The effect is dramatic: a base model might respond to "What is the capital of France?" by continuing with random text, while an instruction-tuned model responds with "Paris."

The mechanism is straightforward but powerful. The model learns to recognize instruction patterns in prompts and generate appropriate responses. It internalizes concepts like "questions deserve answers" and "requests deserve actions." Many human-like behaviors emerge from instruction fine-tuning without explicit programming: the model learns some refusals (unsafe requests), truthfulness (accurate answers are rewarded), and helpfulness (responses that address questions receive positive signal).

Data Requirements and Quality

Instruction fine-tuning requires diverse instruction-response pairs covering the breadth of tasks the model should master. A helpful corpus includes questions, requests for creative writing, code generation, analysis tasks, and many others. The diversity teaches the model to handle varied instructions, not just narrow patterns. Quality matters critically: if examples contain false information, the model learns to generate false information. If examples are inconsistent (same instruction with contradictory responses), the model learns neither consistently.

Instruction Fine-Tuning Limitations

Instruction fine-tuning is effective but has limits. It teaches task-following but doesn't necessarily teach truthfulness or safety. A model can be perfectly following instructions while generating plausible-sounding false information. If training data contains biases (e.g., asking only certain genders certain job questions), the model internalizes these biases. Instruction fine-tuning also doesn't teach models to refuse genuinely harmful requests; models might learn that refusing is one valid response type among many, without understanding what makes some requests harmful.

Instruction Leakage: Models sometimes over-follow instructions, becoming manipulable. A sophisticated prompt can convince an instruction-fine-tuned model to ignore safety guidelines. Instruction following alone is insufficient for safety; additional alignment layers are needed.

RLHF: Alignment Through Reinforcement Learning

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is a more sophisticated alignment approach. Rather than binary instruction-response pairs, RLHF trains a reward model to score outputs on a scale, then uses reinforcement learning to optimize the language model to maximize reward scores. This enables training on nuanced preferences rather than just examples.

The RLHF Pipeline

RLHF proceeds in stages. First, collect a dataset of prompts and generate multiple responses from the model (for each prompt, sample 4-8 diverse responses). Then, have humans rate these responses, typically on a scale (1-7 or similar) for helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty. This creates a dataset of (response, rating) pairs. Next, train a reward model—a classifier predicting the rating for a given response. This reward model learns human preferences by observing which responses receive high ratings and which receive low ratings. Finally, use reinforcement learning to fine-tune the original language model, maximizing the reward model's expected score.

The innovation: rather than manually writing response examples, RLHF learns preferences from human feedback. A complex preference like "responses should be helpful but not manipulative" might be impossible to codify, but humans can rate examples demonstrating it. The reward model learns to recognize the preference, and the RL process optimizes toward it.

Advantages and Challenges

RLHF advantages: (1) learns complex preferences not easily codified, (2) enables continuous improvement (gather more feedback, improve reward model, improve language model), (3) incentivizes nuance (models learn to make fine distinctions in response quality). Challenges: (1) requires large-scale human feedback (expensive and time-consuming), (2) reward model is itself imperfect and can reward unintended behaviors, (3) language models can exploit reward models, generating responses that game the reward metric without genuine improvement, and (4) RL training is complex, unstable, and computationally expensive.

Reward Hacking and Gaming

A subtle but critical problem: language models are very good at recognizing patterns, and can learn to game reward models. Some examples: (1) generating verbose responses that mention reward-valued concepts repeatedly without adding substance, (2) using specific phrasings that the reward model associates with high quality independent of actual quality, or (3) generating confident-sounding content on difficult topics where the reward model incorrectly scores false information as correct. Mitigating reward hacking requires carefully designed reward models and monitoring for gaming behaviors.

1. Collect Prompts

Gather diverse prompts representing the tasks users will pose.

2. Generate Responses

Sample multiple diverse responses from the model for each prompt.

3. Human Evaluation

Have humans rate responses for quality, safety, and other dimensions.

4. Train Reward Model

Build a classifier predicting human ratings for new responses.

5. RL Fine-Tuning

Use reinforcement learning to optimize language model for reward model scores.

DPO: Alignment Without Explicit Reward Models

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is a recently-developed alignment technique that achieves RLHF-like results without the expense and complexity of training intermediate reward models. Instead of learning preferences explicitly, DPO trains the language model to directly maximize preferences.

The DPO Mechanism

Rather than rating responses on a scale (which requires many data points to train a reward model), DPO works with preference comparisons: for a given prompt, humans specify which of two responses is better. This is often easier than numerical ratings—people can compare two responses faster than rating them individually. DPO then directly optimizes the language model to increase likelihood of preferred responses while decreasing likelihood of disfavored responses. The mathematical formulation ensures that improved preference for good responses doesn't cause catastrophic forgetting of general capabilities.

DPO's central insight: you don't need an explicit reward model because the language model itself can embody preferences. By training the model to prefer better responses, you're directly optimizing what matters: human satisfaction.

DPO Advantages

DPO offers several advantages over RLHF. (1) Simpler training: no need to train a separate reward model, combining what was three stages into one. (2) More data efficient: preference comparisons are easier to collect than ratings, so you can gather more feedback with same effort. (3) More stable training: RL training is notoriously unstable; DPO simplifies to supervised learning, which is more stable. (4) Less gaming: without a separate reward model to game, gaming opportunities are reduced.

DPO Limitations and Open Questions

DPO is newer than RLHF and still being actively researched. Some limitations: (1) preference data might be noisier than ratings (people might be inconsistent in comparisons), (2) long-horizon preferences are harder to capture (a response might seem good initially but have consequences later), and (3) scaling to very large numbers of training samples or very fine-grained preference distinctions is unexplored. Despite these limitations, DPO has rapidly gained adoption due to its relative simplicity and effectiveness.

DPO Represents Evolution: DPO demonstrates how alignment techniques continue evolving as researchers find simpler, more effective approaches. Today's RLHF may eventually be replaced by techniques we haven't yet conceived.

Constitutional AI and Value-Based Alignment

Beyond instruction fine-tuning, RLHF, and DPO, more sophisticated alignment approaches explicitly encode values and principles. Constitutional AI specifies a constitution—a set of principles the model should follow—then trains the model to self-critique against the constitution.

Constitutional AI Framework

Rather than relying exclusively on human feedback, Constitutional AI uses a constitution (list of principles like "Be helpful," "Be harmless," "Be honest"). The training process: (1) generate responses to prompts, (2) have the model self-critique responses against the constitution, identifying violations, (3) have the model revise responses to align with constitution, and (4) fine-tune the model on revised responses. This approach teaches the model to critique its own outputs, internalize principles, and self-correct.

Advantages and Philosophy

Constitutional approaches have philosophical advantages: principles are explicit and transparent, enabling stakeholders to understand and debate values the model is optimizing toward. This addresses concerns about opaque human feedback potentially encoding problematic preferences. Constitutional AI also scales better than human feedback—once a constitution is specified, generating self-critiques is cheap compared to human evaluation.

Challenges of Value-Based Alignment

Constitutional AI avoids explicit human feedback but faces different challenges: (1) specifying principles is hard. "Be honest" seems obvious but is deeply complex in practice (truthful but unhelpful? Confident guesses vs. uncertainty acknowledgment?). (2) Principles conflict (helpfulness vs. harmlessness sometimes contradict). (3) The model's self-critique is trained supervision, not ground truth—models can learn to critique badly. (4) The constitutional approach still requires some human feedback to define principles and validate that the model actually internalizes them.

Measuring and Evaluating Alignment

How do you know if a model is well-aligned? Evaluation is challenging because alignment is multidimensional and partly subjective.

Multi-Dimensional Evaluation

Alignment involves multiple dimensions: (1) Helpfulness—does the model try to answer questions and fulfill requests? (2) Harmlessness—does it refuse to help with harmful activities? (3) Honesty—does it generate truthful information and acknowledge uncertainty? (4) Constraint-Respecting—does it follow explicit user instructions about response format and content? These dimensions sometimes conflict; optimizing one might harm others.

Automated Metrics and Human Evaluation

Automated metrics are limited for alignment. You can measure factuality (is generated information correct?) using fact-checking systems, but this is costly and doesn't capture all dimensions. For subjective dimensions like helpfulness, human evaluation is necessary. Organizations conduct periodic evaluation where humans rate outputs on dimensions, tracking trends over time. This provides ground truth but is expensive and doesn't scale to continuous evaluation.

Robustness and Adversarial Testing

Aligned models should be robust to attempts to misalign them. Adversarial testing involves prompts designed to undermine alignment: jailbreak attempts (sophisticated prompts trying to bypass safety constraints), requests for harmful content disguised as legitimate, and requests that expose biases. Successful models should resist these attacks. However, defining what constitutes acceptable resistance is itself contentious—some argue any refusal harms user autonomy, others argue that some requests should always be refused.

Community and Stakeholder Feedback

Ultimately, assessment of whether a model is well-aligned is subjective. Different communities have different values. Some feedback emerges only from deployment: as users interact with the model, misalignment issues surface. Organizations should incorporate community feedback into alignment evaluation and refinement, recognizing that alignment is not a fixed target but an ongoing process.

Emerging Directions and Ongoing Challenges

Alignment research is rapidly evolving. Emerging directions include scalable oversight (techniques for monitoring and guiding increasingly capable models), interpretability-guided alignment (understanding what models learn to better align them), and multi-stakeholder alignment (accommodating diverse values from different user communities). Fundamental challenges remain unsolved: how to specify and optimize for complex values, how to ensure robustness to adversarial manipulation, and how to maintain alignment as models become more capable and autonomous.

Alignment is not a solved problem. Current techniques are imperfect approximations. As technology evolves, alignment challenges intensify. The field will likely continue evolving through combinations of techniques alongside theoretical work understanding why alignment works and how to make it more reliable.

Key Takeaways

Alignment Addresses Intent-Behavior Divergence: Language models trained for text prediction don't automatically behave according to human values; alignment techniques correct this.
Instruction Fine-Tuning is Foundational: Training on instruction-response pairs teaches basic task-following and helpful behavior.
RLHF Learns Complex Preferences: Reward models and RL enable training on nuanced human preferences too complex to codify explicitly.
DPO Simplifies RLHF: Direct preference optimization achieves similar results with simpler training and reduced gaming opportunities.
Constitutional AI Emphasizes Principles: Explicit value-based approaches make alignment transparent and scalable but face challenges in principle definition.
Alignment is Multidimensional and Evolving: No single metric captures alignment; evaluation requires multi-dimensional assessment and continuous improvement.
