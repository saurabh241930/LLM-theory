Bayesian Fundamentals

Updating Beliefs with Evidence

Bayes' Theorem

Frequentist statistics views probability as the long-run frequency of events. Bayesian statistics views probability as a measure of belief that updates as new evidence arrives. This is the absolute foundation of probabilistic ML models.

P(A|B) =
P(B|A) × P(A)
P(B)

1. Prior P(A)

Your initial belief before seeing any data.

Example: What's the chance an email is spam overall? Roughly 40%.

2. Likelihood P(B|A)

If the hypothesis is true, how likely is this evidence?

Example: If an email IS spam, what's the probability it contains the word "Viagra"? High.

3. Posterior P(A|B)

Your updated belief after seeing the evidence.

Example: Given the email contains "Viagra", what is the probability it is spam? The final answer.

Note: The denominator, Evidence P(B), just normalizes the result so probabilities sum to 1.

Naive Bayes Classifier

One of the earliest and most effective spam filtering algorithms. It calculates the posterior probability that a document belongs to a class (e.g., Spam or Ham) based on the words it contains.

Why "Naive"? It assumes that the presence of every feature (word) is independent of every other feature. This is mathematically false ("New" and "York" are highly correlated), but in practice, Naive Bayes works incredibly well for text classification because the combined probabilities still push the final classification in the right direction.

Connection to LLMs

Modern Large Language Models don't use Naive Bayes, but they are fundamentally probabilistic models deeply connected to Bayesian thinking:

  • An LLM calculates the Conditional Probability (Likelihood) of the next token given the prompt context: P(word_N | word_1, word_2 ... word_N-1).
  • Temperature & Top-p Decoding: Manipulate this final probability distribution. A lower temperature sharpens the distribution (favoring high likelihood), while high temperature flattens it (allowing lower probability paths).
  • Bayesian Neural Networks: Advanced active research. Instead of having fixed weights (like GPT-4), a BNN learns a probability distribution for every weight constraint. It can tell you "I'm 90% sure the answer is X, but my uncertainty is high." Standard LLMs confidently hallucinate because they lack proper Bayesian uncertainty measurement.
