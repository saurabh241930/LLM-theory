The Curse of Dimensionality

Why higher dimensions break our intuition

The Core Problem

As the number of features (dimensions) grows, the amount of data needed to generalize accurately grows exponentially. In high-dimensional space, everything is far away from everything else.

1. Sparsity & Distance

Imagine dropping 100 points onto a 1D line: they pack together tightly. Drop those same 100 points into a 3D cube: there is plenty of empty space. Drop them into a 10,000-dimensional space (like an LLM Embedding): the space is so incomprehensibly vast that the concept of "near" starts to lose meaning.

d(x,y) = √Σ(xᵢ - yᵢ)²

In high dimensions, the Euclidean distance between a randomly chosen pair of points converges. Every point appears almost equidistant to every other point. This is why K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) fails completely in high dimensions.

2. Connection to Vector Search / RAG

If distance metrics fail in high dimensions, how does an embedding vector database like Pinecone or Weaviate search through 1,536-dimensional embeddings (like OpenAI ada-002) accurately?

  • Cosine Similarity: We stop measuring the Euclidean straight-line distance, and instead measure the angle between vectors. Angles remain meaningful even in high dimensions.
  • ANN (Approximate Nearest Neighbors): Exact similarity search is O(N). To handle millions of vectors, we build hierarchical graphs (HNSW) to approximate the neighborhood.
  • Dimensionality Reduction: Algorithms like PCA or t-SNE can compress sparse, 10k+ dimension data down to the most important axes, rescuing the model from the curse.

Solutions

  • Feature Selection: Use L1 Regularization (Lasso) to explicitly kill useless features.
  • Feature Extraction: Use PCA or Autoencoders.
  • Increase Data: As dimensions grow, you must exponentially grow your dataset. (Hence why GPT requires trillions of tokens).
