Embedding Model Fine-Tuning

Contrastive Learning, Triplet Loss, and Domain Adaptation

The Limit of Pre-trained Embeddings (MTEB)

The MTEB (Massive Text Embedding Benchmark) is the global leaderboard for embedding models (OpenAI, Cohere, BGE, E5). However, these models are trained on general internet text. If you are building Medical RAG or Legal RAG, the exact vocabulary might be indistinguishable to a standard model. "Myocardial Infarction" and "Heart Attack" might land far apart in vector space if the model didn't read medical journals. You must fine-tune.

Contrastive Learning & Triplet Loss

You do not fine-tune an embedding model by predicting the next word. You fine-tune it by physically dragging vectors around in multi-dimensional space. The industry standard is Triplet Loss.

You need a dataset containing three things:

  1. Anchor: The user's query.
  2. Positive: A document that perfectly answers the query.
  3. Negative: A document that is irrelevant (or dangerously, a "hard negative" that looks relevant but isn't).

Loss = max(0, Distance(Anchor, Positive) - Distance(Anchor, Negative) + Margin)

During backpropagation, the gradients forcefully update the embedding model's weights to pull the Anchor and Positive vectors closer together, while actively repelling the Negative vector away by at least a specified Margin.

The Secret Sauce: Hard Negatives

Training an embedding model with random negatives (e.g., matching a question about "Taxes" against a document about "Dogs") is largely useless. The model already knows they are different.

State-of-the-art embedding fine-tuning relies entirely on Hard Negatives: documents that rank highly in BM25 (share exact keywords) but are semantically the wrong answer. Forcing the model to repel hard negatives is what takes an embedding model from 85% accuracy to 95% accuracy.
