1. Summary

Decision Trees mimic human decision-making by asking a series of binary questions. Each question (node) splits the data into two branches based on the feature that provides the most information gain, until a final prediction (leaf) is reached.

4. The "Splitting" Math

Gini Impurity: Measures how often a randomly chosen element would be incorrectly labeled.
Gini = 1 - Σ (p_i)²

5. Scenarios & Use Cases

🌟 Why use them?

  • Highly Interpretably: "White box" model - you can see exactly why a prediction was made.
  • Handles both numerical and categorical data naturally.
  • Requires very little data preprocessing (scaling is not needed).

🚩 The "Overfitting" Trap

  • Trees love to grow complex and memorize noise.
  • Solution: Pruning (restricting depth) or using Ensembles (Random Forest).

5. Real-World Case Study: Automated Medical Triage

The Problem: A rural clinic with no resident doctor needs to prioritize patients based on symptom urgency.

The Solution: A Decision Tree mimics a medical flowchart:

  • Step 1: Fever > 102°F? (Yes/No)
  • Step 2: Difficulty breathing? (Yes/No)
  • Result: Immediate Referral vs. Schedule Appointment.

Why Decision Trees? Explainability. Doctors and patients trust a "Tree" because it follows the same logical "Rules" a human expert would use, unlike a black-box neural network.
